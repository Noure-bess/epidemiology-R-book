7.1 Training sessions

Similar to SADs, when raters are exposed to a fixed number of reference diagrams to correct and improve accuracy of their visual estimates of severity, if raters are exposed several times to diagrams (or actual images) with known severity values, of varying values, they will also get similar benefits.

7.2 Software

Training sessions have been used in the past and proven to improve rater’s performance. Research on this topic began during the mid-1980s with the arrival of personal computers, which were used to develop computerized images of specific and measured disease severity. Those software, running on DOS or Windows system, included AREAGRAM, DISTRAIN, DISEASE.PRO, ESTIMATE, SEVERITY.PRO and COMBRO (See review by Bock et al. (2021) ). Although training has proven successful, a potential issue with computer training is that the benefits may be short-lived with raters requiring regular re-training.

7.2.1 Online training tools

Recently, there has been developments of training software that runs on the web or smartphones. In Brazil, a system called “Sistema de treinamento de acuidade visual” was initially developed and ran on the web to train raters in the assessment of citrus canker. Current version runs on both iOS and Android platforms.

In Mexico, an application was developed specifically for training raters to visually assess the severity of coffee leaf rust using diagrammatic log-based scales. The system is called Validar-PER and can be found online at here.

7.2.2 Training system made with R

TraineR, developed by the author of this book, is an online app created using R and Shiny. Its purpose is to train users in the assessment of disease severity, which is expressed as the percent area of an organ (leaf or fruit) affected by lesions.

To utilize the app, users can adjust organ shape and color parameters as well as lesion parameters, which include shape, color, number, and size of lesions. These adjustments will create a standard area diagram of ellipsoidal shape.

To start the training, users should first set the number of attempts in the session and click on “generate new”. Then, a diagram is displayed and users should input their estimate as numeric value in percentage (perceived diseased area). The estimate is recorded and shown in a table, together with the actual value to allow comparison between actual and estimate values.

The user proceeds generating new diagrams and providing estimates until reaching the defined number of attempts. After the last attempt, the accuracy, expressed as the Lin’s concordance correlation coefficient is presented to the user. Plots for the relationship between estimates and actual values, as well as the error of the estimates are shown in plots. The full statistics of accuracy is also available.

Currently, the app has some limitations, such as the inability to overlap lesions and a maximum severity of approximately 60%. Despite these limitations, it is a valuable educational and demonstration tool.
